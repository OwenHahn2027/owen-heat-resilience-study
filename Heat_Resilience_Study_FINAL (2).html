Higher-resilience projects cost more to build ($226K to $398K per unit) but reduce resident costs through lower utilities. The Heat Resilience Gap
🌡️

Efficiency ≠ Survivability

A critical finding: Atlanta affordable housing has strong energy efficiency under normal conditions, but almost no design consideration for extreme heat events or power outages. Lenny Koltochnik stated that heat events and power outages "don't play any role whatsoever" in how projects are designed, calling it "business as usual." He noted Japan and South Korea are "a lot more advanced" on this, adding: "Here, we're a little bit backwards."

Lenny Koltochnik, Atlanta Housing
"I don't think the extreme heat events or power outages play any role whatsoever. It's kind of business as usual. For developers, the heat events or power outages really are insignificant... The focus is mostly on efficiency and normal conditions."

Conclusions

Key Findings

1. Resilience is standard, but driven by incentives, not heat preparedness

Most projects score Moderate or above, driven by LIHTC incentives and green certifications. Koltochnik said heat events play "no role whatsoever" in design. However, this may not hold for every developer, as one survey respondent noted their company proactively commits to green building regardless of incentives.

2. Efficiency saves residents money, and the data supports it

Higher resilience tiers show lower utilities ($158 vs $169/mo) and lower cost burden (29.2% vs 30.8%). Roberts confirmed the logic. That said, the sample size is small (20 projects), and other factors like unit size, location, and AMI targeting also affect these numbers.

3. Gap financing is the missing piece

Roberts and survey respondents independently identified gap financing as the critical barrier. This was the most consistent theme across all interviews. Still, Koltochnik offered a different lens, emphasizing that policy reform and resident self-sufficiency matter just as much as funding.

4. Subsidies reach residents, not buildings

Koltochnik said subsidies "are tied to the residents, but not the developers." This is an important distinction, though it may vary by program. Some newer incentive structures, like Georgia's QAP scoring, do tie funding to building features like green certifications.

5. Regulatory duplication adds cost

Developers identified duplicative compliance requirements as a burden. Roberts noted Atlanta's impact and permitting fees "can be a little costly." These views reflect the developer perspective; regulators may see these requirements as necessary safeguards.

6. True heat survivability remains unaddressed

No interviewed professional reported designing for passive survivability during power outages or extreme heat. This finding is significant but comes from a limited sample. Some individual projects, like Herndon Square Phase III with its backup generator and cooling center, are beginning to address this gap.

🏛️

Policy Recommendations

1. Expand gap financing targeted at heat-resilient affordable housing, as called for by both Invest Atlanta and surveyed developers.
2. Streamline regulatory compliance between state HFAs and local housing authorities to reduce duplicative costs.
3. Add heat survivability criteria to Georgia's QAP scoring, incentivizing passive cooling, backup power, and thermal mass beyond normal efficiency.
4. Reframe the cost conversation: utility savings should be weighted as a scoring factor in affordable housing allocation decisions.

Next Steps

Going Deeper

This study establishes the baseline relationship between heat resilience and affordability across 20 Atlanta projects. The next phase of research will go deeper in several directions:

📍
More Projects
Expand the dataset beyond 20 projects to strengthen statistical confidence and capture a wider range of resilience approaches
🔬
Verified Building Data
Obtain actual building specs from developers and architects rather than relying on certification-based estimates
🗣️
More Voices
Interview architects, residents, and utility providers to understand the full picture beyond developers and housing officials

Additional areas to explore include: actual utility bill data from residents in these developments (rather than estimates), a deeper look at how Georgia's QAP scoring could be modified to incentivize heat survivability, and case studies of cities like Phoenix or Houston that are further along in designing for extreme heat in affordable housing. The goal is to move from identifying the gap to proposing specific, implementable solutions for Atlanta.
